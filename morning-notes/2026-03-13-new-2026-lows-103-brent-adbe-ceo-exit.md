## Adobe (ADBE) | Beat on All Metrics. Down 9% Anyway. The CEO Discount.

⚠️ **KEY EVENT** CEO Narayen Stepping Down After 18 Years — The Market Is Pricing Uncertainty

Adobe reported record Q1 results: EPS $6.06 (beat $5.87), revenue $6.40B (beat $6.28B, +12% YoY), AI-first ARR more than tripled, Q2 EPS guidance $5.80-5.85 (above $5.68 estimate), FY26 revenue guidance affirmed at $25.90-26.10B. By every financial metric, this was a strong quarter. But the market is selling the stock −9% because CEO Shantanu Narayen, who built Adobe into a $200B company over 18 years, announced he is stepping down once a successor is appointed. The key risk: AI strategy ownership during a critical inflection year. Who replaces him?

- **Don't panic sell the CEO news.** Narayen will remain as Chair. The financial model is intact. ARR tripling proves Firefly AI is monetising. A new CEO who doubles down on AI could be a re-rating catalyst, not a headwind. But uncertainty = multiple compression short term.
- **The −9% discount is the opportunity window.** ADBE was at trough valuation before this print. Post-CEO-news dip pushes it even further below fair value. Initiate or add in tranches over 2-4 weeks as succession clarity emerges.
- **Q2 guide above consensus.** EPS $5.80-5.85 vs $5.68 est. This is not a company in distress. The CEO transition discount is temporary; the earnings power is structural.

## War Implications | Week 3 — LNG Crisis Joins the Oil Shock

🚨 **NEW ESCALATION** QatarEnergy Force Majeure at Ras Laffan — 20% of Global LNG Offline

The energy crisis just added a second front. QatarEnergy declared force majeure at its Ras Laffan LNG plant, which accounts for approximately 20% of global LNG production. Asian LNG spot prices doubled overnight to $25.40/MMBtu. Pakistan, South Korea, Japan, and Taiwan, all highly import-dependent, are scrambling for alternative supply. European LNG spot markets are also repricing. US Henry Hub natural gas rose 6.1% on LNG export premium spillover. This is no longer just an oil shock. It is a full energy supply shock.

- **Hormuz enters Week 3.** Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei stated again that Hormuz "should remain closed." Iran has now directly attacked commercial tankers, a material escalation beyond the initial blockade. The shipping industry is rerouting around the Cape of Good Hope, adding 10-14 days and $3-5M per voyage.
- **IEA release not working.** The coordinated strategic petroleum reserve release announced Wednesday had minimal price impact. Brent rose anyway. This tells you the supply disruption is being priced as structural, not temporary.
- **Inflation arithmetic is getting worse.** Brent at $103 + Asian LNG at $25 + US NatGas +18% WTD. Goldman's March CPI estimate is being revised up in real time. One desk is now tracking 4.0% headline CPI for March (April release). The Fed cannot move in either direction.
- **US policy response risk.** Multiple Republican senators sent a letter to the White House this week urging emergency action on energy prices. Watch for a Presidential address or executive action on energy that could briefly spike markets.

## War & Geopolitical Sentiment — Our Assessment

The conflict has now crossed three critical thresholds that make near-term resolution materially less likely:

**1. Structural vs. Tactical Disruption.** When the Hormuz blockade began, markets treated it as a tactical event, a negotiating tool with an implicit expiration date. Three weeks later, with direct strikes on commercial tankers, a new Supreme Leader explicitly ruling out reopening, and Qatar LNG now force-majeured, the disruption has become structural. The commodity curve is pricing no normalisation before August 2027. That is not a conflict premium. It is a repricing of the global energy supply map.

**2. The Second Front Problem.** The Qatar LNG force majeure transforms a regional oil disruption into a global energy supply shock. Oil is fungible and partly substitutable via SPR releases and production hikes. LNG is not. Regasification terminal capacity, shipping routes, and contract structures mean Asian import nations cannot pivot quickly. This creates a second inflation vector (via natural gas and electricity prices) that monetary policy has even less ability to address.

**3. Diplomatic Channels Are Closing.** Geneva backchannel talks, which represents the market's last hope for a near-term resolution, are now effectively moot after the tanker attacks. Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei is consolidating power by hardening external posture, a pattern consistent with regime transitions throughout Middle Eastern history. There is no credible peace counterparty visible. Trump's contradictory signals ("ultimate victory" and "it could end soon") reflect domestic political pressure, not a realistic diplomatic pathway.

**Overall Sentiment: Bearish Equities, Structurally Bullish Commodities.** The investment regime has shifted from "risk-on with geopolitical tail risk" to "capital preservation with selective commodity exposure." The only sectors with positive earnings revision momentum are energy, defence, and gold. Everything else is getting downgraded, and the downgrade cycle is accelerating as Brent consolidates above $100. We maintain maximum conviction on our energy/gold/defence overweight and recommend no new equity longs outside those themes until either (a) a credible ceasefire emerges, or (b) the Fed provides explicit forward guidance acknowledging the stagflation trap. Neither is imminent.
